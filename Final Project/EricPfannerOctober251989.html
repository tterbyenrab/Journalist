Bush abortion move ‘no surprise’


By Eric Pfanner

President Bush’s announcement that he would veto a bill providing publicly funded abortions for low-income victims of rape and incest came as no surprise to some Nebraska pro-life and pro-choice activists.

“In the scheme of things, it was expected,” said Susan Hale, a lobbyist for Omaha-Council Bluffs Planned Parenthood. “It doesn’t significantly affect the overall situation.”

The House and Senate passed the bill last week and sent it to the president. Richard Duncan, president of the Rutherford Institute of Nebraska, a pro-life group, said bush’s decision is right because it involves welfare money in the form of Medicaid.

Because pro-life and pro-choice supporters disagree on what types of abortion should be legal, tax money should not be provided, even in the case of rape and incest victims, he said.

Jo Eberly, a coordinator of the Religious Coalition for Abortion Rights, said the pro-choice movement may gain strength as a result of Bush’s decision.

“Sometimes those seeming reverses give quiet people a voice,” she said. She said the president’s decision was not a setback to the pro-choice movement because abortions for rape and incest victims haven’t been funded for eight years.

Julie Lostroh, public relations chairman of the Nebraska Coalition for Life, said the group supports Bush’s decision and will continue lobbying in favor of pro-life.

“The battle just started July 3,” she said, with the Supreme Court’s ruling in Webster vs. Reproductive Health Services, which opened the door for states to limit abortion rights.

Duncan said the decision gave the pro-life movement new life, and could cause several battles over abortion bills in Congress.

“Webster is the real cause why abortion is a front-burner issue,” he said.

Eberly said that although only a small percentage of Americans are active on either side of the abortion issue, many have recently become interested in human rights in general.

“A lot of people are saying we don’t want to be another South Africa,” she said.

Abortion should not be looked at as a moral issue, but rather as a social one, she said. Across-the-board decisions on abortion, she said, don’t give the complete picture.

“Each pregnancy is its own story,” she said.

Eberly said the poor and homeless will most be affected by Bush’s veto. “It will most hurt those without an address; those who can’t be active,” she said.

Before public funds for abortion were cut off eight years ago, she said, 200,000 poor women per year received Medicaid money for abortions.

Lostroh said the rape and incest bill would have “opened the door too far” in providing public funds.

To be feasible, she said, such a bill would have to require rape or incest victims to notify authorities within 72 hours.

The bill does not include a time limit, Lostroh said. As a result, she said, the law would have been abused because many pregnant women could have had paid abortions as long as they “cried rape.”

Eberly said a time limit for reporting rapes is an unreasonable requirement, because many women are afraid to report rapes.

“In many cases, they can hardly admit to themselves that this act has happened,” she said.
